My Teacher Was Wrong About CA Foundation - And I Paid the Price

Written from real CA Foundation exam experience. This article reflects mistakes made during preparation, not theoretical advice.

Writing this feels uncomfortable. Not because it attacks teachers - but because it attacks a belief I trusted blindly.

For a long time, I believed something very deeply: “If I follow my teacher exactly, I will clear CA Foundation.”

I didn’t question strategies. I didn’t analyse results. I didn’t adapt.

I obeyed. And when the result came, obedience did not save me.

This is not a story about blaming a teacher. This is a story about what happens when students outsource thinking in an exam that demands independent execution.

Agar aap CA Foundation ke liye prepare kar rahe ho, toh yeh sirf ek story nahi hai. Yeh wahi problem hai jiske liye daily exam-oriented practice aur exam-hall mindset training zaroori hoti hai.

The Trust Phase: When You Stop Thinking for Yourself

My teacher was experienced. Confident. Respected.

When someone like that speaks, you listen. When they give a plan, you follow.

“Do the full syllabus.” “Don’t skip anything.” “Just write everything you know.”

These instructions sounded safe. Responsible. Logical.

And that is exactly why they were dangerous.

Why Students Believe Teachers Blindly

CA Foundation students are insecure. They are new to professional exams. They are afraid of making wrong decisions.

So when a teacher gives certainty, students grab it.

Certainty feels better than confusion. Even if it’s wrong.

I stopped asking: “Does this work for me?” “Is this practical in the exam?”

I replaced those questions with: “Sir ne bola hai.”

Mistake #1: Treating CA Foundation Like a School Exam

My teacher’s biggest mistake was not understanding how CA Foundation actually works.

The strategy was based on coverage, not conversion.

Finish the syllabus. Revise twice. Write long answers.

This approach works in theory. It collapses in the exam hall.

CA Foundation is not about how much you know. It is about how much you can deliver under pressure.

Mistake #2: “Don’t Skip Anything” Is Terrible Advice

This advice destroyed my attempt.

Instead of strengthening scoring areas, I spread my energy thin.

Weak chapters stayed weak. Strong chapters never became perfect.

The exam does not reward equality. It rewards prioritisation.

But I was never taught how to prioritise.

Mistake #3: No One Taught Me Question Selection

In class, everything was about answering. Nothing was about choosing.

In the exam, choice matters more than content.

I attempted questions emotionally - not strategically.

“Yeh chapter padha hai, likh deta hoon.”

I never asked: “Is this the safest question for marks?”

Mistake #4: Writing More Was Treated as Writing Better

My answers were long. Very long.

I believed length showed effort.

In reality, length often hides lack of clarity.

Examiner ko points chahiye hote hain. Not emotional explanations.

This was never corrected in class.

Mistake #5: Mock Tests Were Optional

Isi wajah se PathCA par mock-style practice ko optional nahi rakha gaya. RTP/MTP based practice aur chapter-wise attempts students ko exam pressure se pehle expose karte hain.

Mock tests were treated like extra practice. Not compulsory training.

I avoided them. No one stopped me.

No one explained that mock tests are where exam fear is created - and then destroyed.

I entered the exam hall untested. The exam tested me brutally.

Mistake #6: Exam Temperament Was Never Discussed

Exam temperament koi motivational video se nahi aata. Yeh sirf tab develop hota hai jab aap time, pressure aur penalties ke saath practice karte ho - exactly jaise real exam mein hota hai.

No one taught me: how to handle panic, how to move on from a bad question, how to manage time loss.

Teachers talk about syllabus. Exams test temperament.

This gap cost me marks I could have easily saved.

The Result Day: When Reality Hits Hard

Yeh article kisi coaching ka review nahi hai, na kisi teacher ke against hai. Yeh sirf ek student ka post-mortem hai - bina sugar-coating ke.

The result didn’t shock me. It disappointed me.

Because deep down, I knew I didn’t perform well - even though I studied hard.

That contradiction hurts the most.

“Itne ghante padhke bhi?”

Yes. Because hours don’t convert themselves into marks.

Yeh baat samajhne mein mujhe ek attempt lag gaya.

Tab laga ki shayad problem mujh mein hi hai.

Par baad mein samajh aaya - problem process mein thi.

Lesson #1: Teachers Teach Subjects, Not Your Exam

This is the hardest truth to accept.

Teachers teach content. Exams test execution.

Execution is your responsibility. Not theirs.

Blind obedience removes accountability - and accountability is required to clear CA.

Lesson #2: One Strategy Never Fits All

If a strategy works for 10 students, it can still fail for you.

Your strengths, speed, memory, writing ability - all matter.

You must customise. Or you will suffer.

Lesson #3: Marks Are Earned by Selection, Not Sincerity

The exam does not know your intention.

It only sees your answers.

Selection of questions, selection of points, selection of time - this is where marks are created.

Lesson #4: Mock Tests Are Non-Negotiable

Mock tests are not about scores.

They are about stress exposure.

The more familiar stress becomes, the weaker it gets.

I learned this too late.

Lesson #5: Respect Teachers, But Don’t Surrender Thinking

This is the balance most students miss.

Respect experience. But verify with performance.

If something isn’t improving your marks, loyalty won’t fix it.

Final Reality

My teacher was not evil. Not careless.

The advice was generic. The exam was not.

I paid the price because I didn’t adapt early.

CA Foundation doesn’t punish bad teachers. It punishes passive students.

Think. Test. Adapt.

Is article ka purpose marks bechna nahi hai. Sirf yeh ensure karna hai ki aap woh galti repeat na karo jo maine ki.

Agar aap CA Foundation ke liye prepare kar rahe ho, toh sirf padhna kaafi nahi hai. Practice karo, test karo, aur apni strategy ko regularly challenge karo.
