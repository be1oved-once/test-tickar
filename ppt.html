What is the Water Act?

The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974 is India's pioneering environmental legislation designed to prevent and control water pollution.

It establishes institutional frameworks including Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs).

Historical Evolution

1972

UN Conference on Human Environment in Stockholm raises global awareness about environmental protection.

1974

Water Act passed by Parliament under Article 252 (legislation for two or more states).

1977

Water Cess Act enacted to provide funds for pollution control boards through levy and collection of cess.

1988

Major amendments strengthen powers of boards and introduce stricter penalties for violations.

2017

National Green Tribunal (NGT) actively enforces water pollution cases with substantial penalties.

Key Provisions & Powers

Water Quality

Setting standards for effluents and establishing laboratories for testing water quality.

Industry Control

Consent mechanism for establishing industries - No Objection Certificate required.

Inspection Rights

Boards can enter premises, inspect processes, and take samples for analysis.

Enforcement

Power to close polluting units, restrict water supply, and levy penalties up to 1 lakh plus imprisonment.

Institutional Framework

Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB)

National apex body under Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change. Coordinates activities, provides technical guidance, and resolves disputes.

State PCB

Implement policies at state level, grant consents, monitor compliance.

NGT

National Green Tribunal - Specialized court for environmental disputes.

Laboratories

State-of-the-art testing facilities for water quality analysis.

Current Challenges

Industrial Pollution

Over 2,500 grossly polluting industries identified. Only 60% have adequate treatment facilities.

Urban Sewage

Cities generate 72,368 MLD sewage but treatment capacity only 31,841 MLD (44%).

Emerging Contaminants

Pharmaceuticals, microplastics, and heavy metals not fully regulated under current framework.

Enforcement Gaps

Weak compliance monitoring. Only 15% of violations result in penalties.

Funding Constraints

PCBs underfunded. Water cess collection declining due to exemptions.

River Pollution

Ganga, Yamuna, and 351 polluted river stretches identified. 45 critically polluted.

Success Stories & Impact

Ganga Action Plan

Launched 1986, renewed as Namami Gange in 2014. Created sewage treatment capacity of 1,500 MLD.

BOD reduced by 40% in Varanasi

Zero Liquid Discharge

Mandatory for textile, tannery, and pharmaceutical clusters in critical areas.

100% wastewater recycling achieved

Online Monitoring

OSWIS (Online Sewerage and Effluent Monitoring System) real-time tracking of 2,000+ industries.

Transparency increased by 80%

Complementary Legislation

Environment Protection Act, 1986

Umbrella legislation providing framework for coordination of activities under various acts.

  • • General powers for Central Govt
  • • Hazardous waste management
  • • Coastal regulation zones

Water Cess Act, 1977

Provides levy and collection of cess on water consumed by industries and local authorities.

  • • 25% rebate for effluent treatment
  • • Funds CPCB and SPCBs
  • • Consumption-based taxation

National Green Tribunal, 2010

Specialized judicial body for effective disposal of environmental cases.

  • • Fast-track environmental justice
  • • Compensation for victims
  • • Strict liability principle

Future Roadmap & Reforms

Amendment 2023

Proposed decriminalization of minor offenses, introduction of compounding fees, and strengthening adjudication mechanisms.

AI Integration

Machine learning models for predictive pollution monitoring and automated compliance checking.

Water Credits

Introduction of market-based mechanisms for water quality trading and offset programs.

Digital Twin

Creating digital replicas of major river basins for real-time simulation and management.

Public Participation

Citizen science initiatives and community monitoring programs for local water bodies.

Circular Economy

Mandatory water recycling norms for new industries and incentives for zero discharge technologies.

Conclusion

The Water Act, 1974 remains the cornerstone of India's water pollution control framework. While significant challenges persist in enforcement and compliance, the Act has evolved through amendments, judicial interventions, and complementary legislation to address emerging environmental threats.
